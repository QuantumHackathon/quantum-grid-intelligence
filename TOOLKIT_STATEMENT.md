## Software Toolkit Statement — Pytket, Guppy & Quantinuum Ecosystem

We implemented MA-QAOA (Multi-Angle Warm-Started QAOA — independent γ per edge, independent β per qubit) using a pure NumPy/SciPy statevector simulation for rapid prototyping and exact reproducibility, with a parallel Pytket circuit builder for Quantinuum emulator submission. We then re-expressed the optimized MA-QAOA circuit in **Guppy** (Quantinuum's Python-embedded quantum language), compiled it to **HUGR**, and executed it for real on **Quantinuum Nexus** against the Nexus-hosted **Selene emulator** — moving beyond circuit construction to genuine shot-based execution on Quantinuum's own infrastructure.

**What worked:** The statevector approach provided exact, noiseless QAOA simulation for 8 qubits without external quantum SDK dependencies, ensuring full reproducibility. Vectorizing that simulation with NumPy (reshape + matrix ops instead of per-basis-state Python loops) cut a full benchmark run from ~17 minutes to ~76 seconds once MA-QAOA's larger parameter count made the original loop-based version too slow to iterate on comfortably. SciPy's L-BFGS-B optimizer proved effective for parameter optimization despite the jump from 2 to up to 51 parameters. NetworkX for graph construction and CVXPY for SDP-based Goemans-Williamson baseline were straightforward and reliable. Guppy's Python-embedded syntax made it possible to reuse our existing graph/optimization code directly and express the full per-edge/per-qubit circuit in well under 100 lines. `qnexus`'s login → project → upload → execute-job → results flow was simple to script end-to-end, and the resulting Nexus execution on the Selene emulator (5000 shots) reproduced the idealized statevector ratio almost exactly (r=0.987 vs r=0.987 idealized), validating that the Guppy circuit is a faithful re-expression of the NumPy one.

**What did not:** Four distinct issues surfaced — one from our own optimizer configuration, three from the Guppy/Nexus toolchain:

- **No analytic gradient for L-BFGS-B.** We never passed `jac` to `scipy.optimize.minimize`, so SciPy estimates gradients by finite differences — a cost that scales directly with parameter count (17 at p=1, up to 51 at p=3). Combined with MA-QAOA's non-convex landscape, most p=2/p=3 runs did not formally converge within `maxiter=300`. Lesson for future work: provide an analytic or parameter-shift gradient, or switch to an optimizer designed for expensive/noisy gradients (e.g. SPSA).
- **Guppy's gate convention silently differs from ours.** Guppy's `zz_phase(θ) = exp(-iθ/2·Z⊗Z)` differs in sign and scale from our NumPy cost unitary. Getting this wrong doesn't crash anything — it produces a valid-looking circuit that quietly implements the wrong physics. Caught only by comparing Nexus output numerically against the idealized statevector, not by the compiler.
- **Matrix-product order is reversed from circuit order.** Our custom mixer decomposes as `Ry(θ)·Rz(2β)·Ry(-θ)` — as a matrix product, but the *rightmost* factor is applied *first* in time on real hardware. Same failure mode as above: a plausible-looking circuit that's silently wrong. We validated the decomposition numerically (10⁻⁸ agreement) before trusting it in the compiled circuit.
- **Guppy's `py(...)` can't re-unroll a variable from an outer unrolled loop.** Generalizing from one global γ/β to per-edge/per-qubit angles, our first attempt nested `for edge_angles in py(layers): for u,v,ang in py(edge_angles):` — Guppy's compiler rejected it: once a value comes out of an outer `py(...)` loop, it's a Guppy value, not a raw Python object `py()` can re-evaluate. Fixed by flattening every gate (across all layers) into one ordered list of `(op_code, qubit_a, qubit_b, angle)` tuples, dispatched with plain `if`/`elif` inside a single loop. Undocumented in Guppy 0.21.x's published examples.

**What was missing:** We now have Nexus access and executed on the Selene emulator, but Selene's default configuration is a **noiseless** statevector backend — we did not exercise Quantinuum's noise models (`QSystemErrorModel`, `DepolarizingErrorModel`) or submit to physical hardware, so hardware-realistic noise resilience remains untested. The classical optimization of γ*/β* still runs entirely against the idealized NumPy simulator; those angles are baked into the Nexus circuit as fixed constants rather than being optimized in a closed loop against real Nexus shots (as Quantinuum's own QAOA examples do with COBYLA). A production version would extend this to noisy-emulator and closed-loop Nexus optimization, and to Quantinuum's larger Helios-class systems for topologies beyond 8 nodes.
